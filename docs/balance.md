# Too much documentation can also be a problem

A real failure mode:

- a project begins with an extremely formal specification
- schedules, contingencies, detailed requirements
- written before anyone has built or tested a prototype

That level of rigor can be appropriate in aerospace/defense contexts where:
- requirements are stable,
- risk is high,
- and the project is essentially a product program.

In exploratory engineering/science, it can backfire:
- the real requirements aren’t known yet,
- stakeholders haven’t touched a prototype,
- and the team learns fastest by building and iterating.

## A better pattern for exploratory work

1. **Prototype early**
2. **Document what you learned** (what worked, what failed, why)
3. **Add formality as the design stabilizes**

In other words:

> Start lightweight, then increase rigor as uncertainty drops.
